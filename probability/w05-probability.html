  1. The table shows a summary of data from the Pecs Bar and Gym, relating to session attended and whether the customer came to the session by car or not.

    Customer Frequencies by Session and Journey
    Session Car No Car Total
    Free Weights3?4
    Cardio Machines4?5
    Fitness Class?24
    Bar??3
    Total10616

    1. Complete the two-way table.

      Show Solution

      Use the row and column totals to find the missing values.
      Free Weights (No Car): 4 - 3 = 1
      Cardio (No Car): 5 - 4 = 1
      Fitness Class (Car): 4 - 2 = 2
      Bar (Car): Total Car (10) - 3 - 4 - 2 = 1
      Bar (No Car): 3 - 1 = 2

    2. A person is selected at random. Find the probability that:

      1. they went to the fitness class
      2. they came by car
      3. they used the cardio machines and came by car
      4. they did not use free weights and did not come by car
      Show Solution

      i. Total in Fitness Class is 4. Total people is 16. P(Fitness Class) = 4/16 = 1/4.
      ii. Total came by Car is 10. Total people is 16. P(Car) = 10/16 = 5/8.
      iii. Number in Cardio and Car is 4. P(Cardio and Car) = 4/16 = 1/4.
      iv. Total not using Free Weights = 16 - 4 = 12. Total not by car = 6. The number of people in this group is (Cardio, No Car) + (Fitness, No Car) + (Bar, No Car) = 1 + 2 + 2 = 5. P = 5/16.

    3. Given that a randomly selected person came by car, what is the probability they went to the fitness class?

      Show Solution

      This is a conditional probability. The total is now only the people who came by car (10). Of those 10, 2 went to the fitness class.

      Answer: P(Fitness | Car) = 2/10 = 1/5.

  2. The Pecs Bar and Gym carry out a larger-scale study of its facility usage. The diagram below summarises some of the findings.

    1. What is the name given to this type of diagram?

    3. What is the probability that a randomly selected person used the pool?

      Show Solution

      Total who used the Pool = 34 (Pool only) + 46 (Both) = 80. P(Pool) = 80 / 250.

      Answer: 8/25 or 0.32

    4. What is the probability that a randomly selected person used both the pool and the bar?

      Show Solution

      The number in the overlapping section is 46. P(Pool and Bar) = 46 / 250.

      Answer: 23/125 or 0.184

    5. What is the probability that a randomly selected person did not use the bar?

      Show Solution

      Total who used the Bar = 120 + 46 = 166. Total who did not use the Bar = 250 - 166 = 84. P(Not Bar) = 84 / 250.

      Answer: 42/125 or 0.336

    6. What is the probability that a randomly selected person used the pool or the bar?

      Show Solution

      P(Pool or Bar) = P(Pool) + P(Bar) - P(Pool and Bar). P(Bar) = (120+46)/250 = 166/250. P(Pool or Bar) = (80/250) + (166/250) - (46/250) = 200/250.

      Answer: 4/5 or 0.8

    7. Given that a person used the bar, what is the probability that they used the pool?

      Show Solution

      The total is now only the people who used the bar (120 + 46 = 166). Of these, 46 also used the pool. P(Pool | Bar) = 46 / 166.

      Answer: 23/83 or approx 0.277

    8. Are the events of attending the pool and using the bar independent?

      Show Solution

      For events to be independent, P(Pool | Bar) must equal P(Pool). We found P(Pool | Bar) = 23/83 â‰ˆ 0.277. We found P(Pool) = 8/25 = 0.32. Since these are not equal, the events are not independent.

      Answer: No, they are not independent.

    9. Two people are randomly selected from the group. What is the probability that:

      1. they are two people who both used the pool?
      2. at least one of the two people used the pool?
      Show Solution

      i. P(1st used Pool) = 80/250. P(2nd used Pool | 1st used Pool) = 79/249. Total P = (80/250) * (79/249).
      ii. It's easiest to calculate the opposite: P(neither used Pool) and subtract from 1. P(1st did not use Pool) = (250-80)/250 = 170/250. P(2nd did not use Pool) = 169/249. P(both did not use Pool) = (170/250) * (169/249). P(at least one) = 1 - [(170/250) * (169/249)].

      Answer: i) approx 0.101, ii) approx 0.539

  3. Customers can use lockers while enjoying the facilities... There are 90 green tagged keys, numbered 1 to 90. There are 60 red tagged keys, numbered 1 to 60... The receptionist... pulls out a key.

    1. What is the probability that they get a green tagged key?

      Show Solution

      Total keys = 90 + 60 = 150. Number of green keys = 90. P(Green) = 90/150.

      Answer: 3/5 or 0.6

    2. What is the probability that they get a key numbered over 50?

      Show Solution

      Green keys numbered over 50 = 90 - 50 = 40. Red keys numbered over 50 = 60 - 50 = 10. Total keys over 50 = 40 + 10 = 50. P(>50) = 50/150.

      Answer: 1/3

  4. The company gets its equipment from two suppliers, supplier X and supplier Y. Evidence suggests that supplier X's equipment has a 5% chance of failing in the first year. The figure for supplier Y is 8%. (Assume the gym gets 50% of its equipment from each supplier).

    1. By making use of a tree diagram, or otherwise, find the probability that a randomly selected piece of equipment:

      1. is from supplier X and fails in the first year
      2. fails in the first year
      Show Solution

      i. P(X and Fails) = P(X) * P(Fails | X) = 0.5 * 0.05 = 0.025.
      ii. P(Fails) = P(X and Fails) + P(Y and Fails) = (0.5 * 0.05) + (0.5 * 0.08) = 0.025 + 0.040 = 0.065.

    2. Given that a piece of equipment fails during the first year, find the probability:

      1. it was from supplier X
      2. it was from supplier Y
      Show Solution

      This uses Bayes' theorem. We know P(Fails) = 0.065 from part b.
      i. P(X | Fails) = P(X and Fails) / P(Fails) = 0.025 / 0.065.
      ii. P(Y | Fails) = P(Y and Fails) / P(Fails) = 0.040 / 0.065.

      Answer: i) approx 0.385, ii) approx 0.615

  5. The bar staff at the Pecs Bar and Gym have a game they play with customers. The bar staff member picks a card at random from a standard 52-card deck and will offer a free drink if the customer can guess what it is.

    1. What is the probability that the customer guesses the correct card?

      Show Solution

      There is one correct card out of 52 possibilities.

      Answer: 1/52

    The customer is unlikely to win. To help the customer, they are allowed to ask a single â€˜yes/noâ€™ question, from the list below, which the bar member will answer honestly, before they guess. The choices of questions are:

    • Is the card a red card?
    • Is the card a club?
    • Is the card the 5 of diamonds?

    1. Work out the single, overall probability of guessing correctly for each of these initial questions.

      Show Solution

      We calculate the total probability of winning for each scenario:

      "Is it red?":
      P(Win) = P(Win | Yes)P(Yes) + P(Win | No)P(No)
      P(Win) = (1/26 * 26/52) + (1/26 * 26/52) = 1/52 + 1/52 = 2/52 = 1/26.

      "Is it a club?":
      P(Win) = P(Win | Yes)P(Yes) + P(Win | No)P(No)
      P(Win) = (1/13 * 13/52) + (1/39 * 39/52) = 1/52 + 1/52 = 2/52 = 1/26.

      "Is it the 5 of diamonds?":
      P(Win) = P(Win | Yes)P(Yes) + P(Win | No)P(No)
      P(Win) = (1 * 1/52) + (1/51 * 51/52) = 1/52 + 1/52 = 2/52 = 1/26.

      Answer: The overall probability of winning is 1/26 for all three questions.
